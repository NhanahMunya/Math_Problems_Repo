§0 Sample Space

The experiment consists of two independent rolls of a fair six-sided die. Each roll produces a result in $\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$. Since order matters — the first and second roll are distinct stages — each elementary outcome is an ordered pair $(i,j)$ where $i$ is the result of the first die and $j$ is the result of the second.

$$\Omega = \{(i,j) \mid i,j \in \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}\}, \quad |\Omega| = 36$$

In the grid representation, rows correspond to the first die (value $i$) and columns correspond to the second die (value $j$). An event is any subset $A \subseteq \Omega$. Cells marked ✕ belong to the event; empty cells do not.

Here $k = 3$ even values, so the sub-grid covers $\tfrac{3}{6} \times \tfrac{3}{6} = \tfrac{1}{4}$ of the full grid — consistent with $P = \tfrac{9}{36} = \tfrac{1}{4}$. The even and odd rows/columns interleave symmetrically, making the sub-grid easy to spot as a regular checker pattern.
A.4 At least one die shows 6

Hover over any cell to see its outcome

"At least one" is the union of two simpler events. Let $R$ = "first die shows 6" (entire row 6) and $C$ = "second die shows 6" (entire column 6). We want $R \cup C$:

$$|R \cup C| = |R| + |C| - |R \cap C| = 6 + 6 - 1 = 11$$

The overlap $R \cap C = \{(6,6)\}$ is counted in both row and column, so we subtract it once.

Event D — at least one die = 6 (L-shape)
↕ first die (rows) ↔ second die (cols)
|D| = 11 outcomes  |  Complement: $5 \times 5 = 25$, so $36 - 25 = 11$ ✓
Geometric Observation The event forms an L-shape — the union of the bottom row and the rightmost column. The corner $(6,6)$ belongs to both and is counted once, which is why the count is $6 + 6 - 1 = 11$ rather than 12. Equivalently, the complement "neither die shows 6" is a $5 \times 5$ inner square (25 outcomes), confirming $36 - 25 = 11$.

"Exactly one" means the two cases are mutually exclusive — we must exclude the corner $(1,1)$ where both dice show 1. Formally this is the symmetric difference of "first die = 1" and "second die = 1":

This is the geometric counterpart of the set-theoretic distinction between union ($A \cup B$) and symmetric difference ($A \triangle B$).

§∞ Key Insights

Rows × Columns

Constraints on only the first die fill entire rows; constraints on only the second die fill entire columns; constraints on both produce rectangular sub-grids.

Geometry → Structure

Diagonal stripes ↔ constant sums. Triangles ↔ inequality events. Rectangular blocks ↔ independent conjunctive constraints. L-shapes ↔ "at least one" unions.

At Least vs. Exactly One

Both produce L-shapes. "At least one" includes the corner; "exactly one" excludes it. This is the geometric form of $A \cup B$ versus $A \triangle B$.

Complement Shortcut

For "at least one die = 6" the complement is a tidy $5 \times 5$ block (25 outcomes): $36 - 25 = 11$. Complements often have simpler geometric shapes.

Symmetry of Dice

Swapping $i \leftrightarrow j$ reflects the grid across the main diagonal. The lower triangle ($i > j$) and upper triangle ($i < j$) are always equal in size: 15 outcomes each.

Sub-grid Probability

A $k \times k$ sub-grid from a size-$k$ restriction on each die covers $k^2/36$ of $\Omega$. For even values, $k=3$: probability $9/36 = 1/4$.
